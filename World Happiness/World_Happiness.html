World Happiness 2017

Description:

This data set, World Happiness 2017, provides national happiness scores and various life factors of 155 countries reported in 2017.

Variables:

  • Country - Name of the country
  • Happiness Rank - Rank of the country in Happiness Score
  • Happiness Score - National average of the responses to the Cantril ladder question: "Please imagine a ladder, with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?" (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 9)
  • Whisker high - the upper bound of the 95% confidence interval of the Happiness Score.
  • Whisker low - the lower bound of the 95% confidence interval of the Happiness Score.
  • GDP per Capita - Gross Domestic Product per Capita. "GDP per capita is in terms of Purchasing Power Partity (PPP) adjusted to constant 2011 international dollars, taken from the World Development Indicators (WDI), released by the World Bank in August 2016." (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 17)
  • Family - "[...] the national average of the binary responses (either 0 or 1) to the Gallup World Poll question: 'If you were in trouble, do you have relatives or friends you can count on to help you whenever you need them, or not?'" (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 17)
  • Life Expectancy - Healthy life expectancy at birth based on data from the World Health Organisation and World Development Indicators.
  • Freedom - "[...] the national average of binary responses to the Gallup World Poll question: 'Are you satisfied or dissatisfied with your freedom to choose what you do with your life?'" (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 17)
  • Generosity - "[...] the residual of regressing the national average of GWP [Gallup World Poll] responses to the question 'Have you donated money to a charity in the past month?' on GDP per Capita" (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 17)
  • Government Corruption - "Perceptions of corruption are the average of binary answers to two GWP [Gallup World Poll] questions: 'Is corruption widespread throughout the government or not?' and 'Is corruption widespread within businesses or not?'" (Helliwell, Layard, & Sachs, 2017, p. 17)

References:

Helliwell, J., Layard, R., & Sachs, J. (eds., 2017). World Happiness Report 2017. Sustainable Development Solutions Network. Retrieved from https://worldhappiness.report/ed/2017/.

The World Happiness Report is published annually by the United Nations (https://worldhappiness.report/). Data from other years are available, for example, at https://www.kaggle.com/mathurinache/world-happiness-report.

Bayesian Linear Regression

In this analysis, we assess which of the six life factors predict Happiness Score. Because there are six potential predictors, there are 26 = 64 possible models. We selected uniform model prior probabilities so that each of the models under consideration is equally likely before seing the data.

We limit the number of models shown to ten best models (according to their posterior model probability) to have a better overview of the most relevant models. The Model Comparison table shows the summary of those models.


Column P(M) shows the prior model probability, which is equal for all models. Column P(M|data) shows the posterior model probability, given the data. The model with the highest posterior model probability is the one that includes GDP per Capita, Family, Life Expectancy, and Freedom into the predicton, and gets almost 0.5 of the posterior probability mass. This is also the model for which there is the most evidence: the posterior model odds are about 62.5 larger than the prior model odds for the model (as indicated by the column BFM). In fact, only four models out of the 64 possible models became more plausible after seeing the data, as only the four best models have BFM higher than one. Column BF01 enables comparisons between individual models. Specifically, we selected the option "compare to the best model" and "BF01", thus BF01 tells us how many times more likely are the data to occur under the best model compared to the model under consideration. For example, we see that the data are about 1.7 times more likely to occur under the best model than under the model that adds Government Corruption on top of the predictors that are already included in the best model. Notice that the best model according to the posterior model probability is the model that does not explain the most variance in the data (R2).
